AGOHO

(Casuarina equisetifolia)

Agoho is a large evergreen tree native to tropical and subtropical regions. Described by Linnaeus in 1759, it features a narrowly pyramidal crown and can reach up to 20 meters in height. The tree's bark is brown and rough, while its branchlets are slender, about 20 centimeters long, and composed of numerous joints. The foliage resembles horse hair, with green to grey-green twigs bearing minute scale-leaves. Unlike many Casuarina species that are dioecious, C. equisetifolia is monoecious, producing both male and female flowers on the same tree. The fruit is an oval woody structure resembling a conifer cone, which opens when ripe to disperse seeds by water. These seeds, which can only grow in hot sand near the seashore, sprout and form dense thickets.

COMMON USES

Agoho is utilized in various ways across different cultures. Its seeds are roasted for salt, and the leaves and green fruit are chewed to alleviate thirst. In traditional medicine, the tree's branches are used as a diuretic, while its leaves, bark, and decoctions are employed to treat a range of ailments including colic, stomach aches, dysentery, and respiratory issues. The bark's infusion is also used for coughs, asthma, and diabetes, with specific applications varying by region. Additionally, the tree serves practical purposes: its wood is highly valued as fuel, producing high-quality charcoal, and its dense hardwood is used in construction for making timber, poles, and tool handles. The bark is rich in tannins and used for tanning leather, while the wood is processed into paper pulp and rayon fibers.
