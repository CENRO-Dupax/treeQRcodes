GOVERNOR'S PLUM

(Flacourtia indica)

The governor's plum tree (Flacourtia indica) is a small to medium-sized, thorny tree or shrub, typically growing between 3 to 15 meters in height. It has a dense, spreading crown with sharp spines on its branches. The leaves are simple, ovate to elliptical, and alternately arranged, with finely serrated edges. They are glossy green on the upper surface and paler underneath. Flowers are small, white to greenish-yellow, and typically grow in clusters. The tree produces round or oval fruits, about 2 to 3 centimeters in diameter, which turn dark purple to black when ripe. The fruits are edible, often sweet with a tart taste, and contain several seeds. The governor's plum is native to tropical regions and is often found in thickets or along forest edges. It is used for both food and traditional medicine.

COMMON USES

The Governor's Plum is valued for its edible fruits, which are sweet and tart, often eaten fresh or used in jams, jellies, and beverages. In traditional medicine, its bark, leaves, and roots are used to treat various ailments, including fever, diarrhea, dysentery, and skin conditions. It is also applied to wounds, sores, and ulcers for healing, and root extracts are sometimes used for snake bites. Known for its antioxidant and antimicrobial properties, the plant plays a role in treating rheumatism and other conditions. Additionally, the durable wood is used in construction, and the thorny branches make it ideal for hedges and fencing.
