ANTIPOLO

(Artocarpus blancoi)

Artocarpus blancoi, a large tree belonging to the Moraceae family, is endemic to the Philippines and threatened by habitat loss. The genus name "Artocarpus" comes from the Greek words for bread (artos) and fruit (karpos). Antipolo City in Rizal, Philippines, is named after the Antipolo tree, which once grew abundantly in the area. The tree can grow up to 30 meters tall and has simple, spirally arranged leaves with lobes. Its male inflorescence is smooth and cylindric, while the fruit is ellipsoid, ripening to yellow or orange-brown, with spiculate appendages and ellipsoid seeds.

COMMON USES

The Antipolo tree has various uses. Its fruits and seeds are cooked and eaten, and the Ayta people of Pampanga and Zambales use its leaves and young fruits as food, often cooking them with coconut milk. Medicinally, a bark decoction is used for strangularia, while the ashes of burned leaves are mixed with oil to treat skin infections. The wood is used for carving and light construction, while the fibers are utilized for making paper and weak ropes. Dry leaves are burned as a mosquito repellent, and the tree's sticky sap is used by hunters to trap small birds.
