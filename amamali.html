AMAMALI

(Leea guinensis)

Amamali is a smooth or nearly smooth shrub or small tree, typically 3 to 5 meters tall. Its leaves are compound, either three or four times pinnate, and measure 50 to 80 centimeters in length. The leaflets are elliptic-ovate to oblong lanceolate, ranging from 6 to 15 centimeters long, with toothed margins, pointed tips, and rounded or slightly pointed bases. The plant produces large cymes of flowers, up to 50 centimeters in diameter, each flower being five-parted and about 3 millimeters long. Flowers open gradually, with the stalks and calyx being red and the petals pale yellow. The fruit is a dark red, depressed-globose berry approximately 8 millimeters in diameter

COMMON USES

In traditional medicine, the Amamali plant is utilized for various therapeutic purposes. A decoction of its roots, branches, and leaves is employed for wound healing. In Thailand, the root is used to treat diarrhea and hallucinations. In the southern Western Ghats, the leaf juice mixed with coconut milk is administered three times daily to treat dysentery with blood discharge. The plant is also known as Leea guineense and is used for managing hypertension. In West Africa and Guinea-Bissau, it is applied in the treatment of epilepsy. Additionally, the plant is used to address an enlarged spleen in children, pregnancy detection, toothache, gonorrhea, diarrhea, dysentery, and acts as a diuretic.
