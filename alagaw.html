ALAGAW

(Premna odorata)

is a species of flowering plant in the Lamiaceae family, with its genus Premna named from the Greek word premon, meaning tree stump, due to the twisted trunks of its first-discovered species. The specific epithet odorata refers to its fragrant nature. Known locally as siargaw or saliargaw, the island of Siargao in the Philippines is named after this plant. The species is a small, hairy tree ranging from 3 to 8 meters in height. Its leaves are ovate to broadly ovate, 10 to 20 centimeters long, with a broad, rounded, or heart-shaped base and pointed tips. The underside of the leaves is typically covered with short hairs and aromatic when crushed. It bears greenish-white or nearly white flowers, 4 to 5 millimeters long, on terminal inflorescences (cymes) measuring 8 to 20 centimeters in diameter. The fruit is fleshy, dark purple, rounded, and about 5 millimeters in diameter.

COMMON USES

Premna odorata has various uses both culinary and medicinal. In cooking, young leaves are used in dishes like "paksiw" and "bopis." Traditionally, in the Philippines, a sugared decoction of the leaves with a bit of "calamansi" is consumed as tea to relieve coughs and loosen phlegm. The Ayta community of Dinalupihan uses a decoction of leaves and shoots directly on wounds to aid healing, while the Ati Negrito apply boiled leaves externally for postpartum bleeding. The Ilongot-Egongot community of Aurora applies leaves and stem-bark to wounds, and decoctions of fresh leaves are used for vaginal irrigation, treating fever, colds, coughs, bronchitis, fever blisters, and stomachaches. A local patent preparation called "Kochoi" is claimed to benefit tuberculosis. Additionally, leaf decoctions help with flatulence in adults and are used in children for gas pains, while crushed leaves are applied to the forehead for headaches. Roots are chewed for cardiac issues, and various plant parts are used for sudorific, pectoral, and carminative purposes. The leaves are also included in the herbal Filipino tea blend known as "pito-pito" and used in ethno-veterinary practices for fumigating poultry houses to eliminate lice and ticks. The leaves and flowering tops are utilized as a vaginal wash due to their antiseptic properties, making them valuable in bath-care products.
