ANUBING

(Artocarpus ovatus)

Anubing is a deciduous tree that can grow up to 30 meters tall with a trunk diameter reaching about 100 centimeters. The leaves are oblong or subelliptic, measuring 20 to 30 centimeters long and 6 to 10 centimeters wide, and are hairy with pointed or slightly heart-shaped bases. The petioles are 1 to 2 centimeters long. The tree produces male spikes that are pear-shaped and 1 to 2 centimeters long, while female heads are rounded and nearly 2 centimeters in diameter. The fruit is irregularly rounded, about 10 centimeters long, and has brownish, hairy appendages or tails; it starts green and matures to yellow or brown. Inside the fruit, there are 10 to 30 ellipsoid seeds embedded in whitish, gummy flesh.

COMMON USES

The Anubing tree has various uses. The fruit is reported as edible, although some consider the pulp inedible. The seeds are also edible and can be boiled or roasted. Traditionally, the bark is used to alleviate stomach aches. The wood of the anubing tree is prized for its strength and durability, making it suitable for house posts, telegraph poles, and bridges. It is also used in furniture making, light carpentry, interior joinery, paneling, and for crafting boxes and crates; in Malaysia, it is used for high-quality coffins. Additionally, the latex from the tree can be processed into anubing gum and has potential as a chewing gum base. The fruit and leaves are utilized as fodder for animals.
