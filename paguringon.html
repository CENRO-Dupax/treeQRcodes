PAGURINGON

(Cratoxylum sumatranum)

Cratoxylum, a genus in the Hypericaceae family, is named from the Greek meaning "strong wood," highlighting its timber quality. One species, Guyong-guyong, is a smooth, branched tree that can reach heights of 20 to 35 meters with a trunk diameter of up to 40 cm. Its elliptical to ovate-oblong leaves are 4 to 18 cm long, and the bark is dark brown, peeling off in thin strips, with an inner bark that ranges from pink to orange-brown. The tree produces small, red flowers in terminal or axillary panicles, and its fruit is a smooth, dark reddish-brown ellipsoid capsule, about 1 cm long, containing numerous winged seeds. The tree is deciduous and known for its durable wood.

COMMON USES

The stems of Cratoxylum are traditionally used in ethnobotany as an energy drink and aphrodisiac. In the Philippines, a decoction made from the roots, leaves, and bark is given to women postpartum as a protective remedy. The Subanen tribe of Zamboanga del Sur applies young leaves to the forehead as an anticonvulsant. Crushed leaves are placed on the forehead and chest to treat colds, and a decoction of the bark and leaves is used internally as an abortifacient and a galactagogue. Young leaves are chewed for cough relief, and in Indonesia, the plant is used to treat infections, dysentery, colds, and toothaches. The wood is valued for interior construction, furniture, carving, implements, and fuel, including charcoal production
