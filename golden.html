GOLDEN SHOWER

(Cassia fistula)

The golden shower tree, also known as the Indian laburnum, is a flowering tree native to the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia, renowned for its bright yellow, cascading flower clusters that resemble a "golden shower." Widely used as an ornamental plant in gardens and along streets, it is valued for its vibrant appearance. Traditionally, the tree has been used in folk medicine, with its fruit pulp serving as a laxative and other parts, like leaves and bark, believed to have therapeutic properties, though its seeds are toxic if ingested. In Thailand, where it is called "Ratchaphruek," the golden shower tree is the national flower and symbolizes the nation in various ceremonies and emblems.

COMMON USES

The golden shower tree is commonly used both ornamentally and medicinally. Known for its bright yellow, cascading flowers, it is widely planted in gardens, parks, and along streets for its striking appearance, adding aesthetic value to landscapes. Traditionally, various parts of the tree are used in folk medicine: the fruit pulp serves as a natural laxative, while the leaves, bark, and roots are believed to have antibacterial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory properties, treating conditions such as constipation, skin infections, and digestive issues. The tree also holds cultural significance in Thailand, where it is the national flower and symbolizes royalty and prosperity in ceremonies. Additionally, it provides environmental benefits, such as preventing soil erosion and improving soil quality, though its seeds are toxic if ingested.
