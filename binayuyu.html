BINAYUYU

(Antidesma ghaesembilla)

Binayuyu is a genus of tropical plants in the family Phyllanthaceae, first described by Linnaeus in 1753, with 101 accepted species as of 2021. The name Antidesma comes from the Greek words meaning "against" and "band," while the species epithet originates from a Sri Lankan plant name. One species, binayuyo, is a small deciduous tree that grows up to 16 meters tall with light grey bark and simple, alternate leaves. Its male flowers are yellowish-green and grouped into branched spikes, while female flowers grow in racemes. The tree produces small, dark purple drupes that are agreeably acidic. The species is characterized by pubescent young twigs, needle-like stipules, and both male and female flowers having 4 or 5 sepals.

COMMON USES

The Binayuyu plant has various uses, with its leaves and fruits being edible. The fully ripe fruits can be eaten raw, cooked, pickled, or made into jams and jellies, while the shoots are used as a sour vegetable or spice. In traditional medicine, its leaves are used in India and Thailand for treating headaches, fever, and blood nourishment, while in Vietnam and China, it's used for coughs, rheumatism, and menstrual disorders. The fruit has purgative properties, and its stems are used to stimulate menstruation. Additionally, the plant is used for breast milk production, wound dressing, and in herbal remedies for diabetes and uterine pain. Its wood is employed in construction, and in some cultures, it serves as a calendar plant, signaling harvest time. It also produces a black dye used for sunhats in East Kalimantan.
