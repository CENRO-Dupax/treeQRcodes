SAMPALOK

(Tamarindus indica Linn.)

The sampalok tree (Tamarindus indica) is a large, nearly glabrous tree that grows between 12 to 25 meters in height. Its leaves are evenly pinnate, measuring 6 to 10 centimeters long, with 20 to 40 closely spaced, oblong leaflets that are 1 to 2 centimeters long. The tree produces racemes, mostly axillary but sometimes panicled, reaching 5 to 10 centimeters in length. The calyx is about 1 centimeter long with a turbinate tube and lanceolate teeth, two of which are connate. The petals are yellowish with pink stripes, obovate-oblong, and less than 1 centimeter long, with only the three upper petals fully developed. The stamens are monadelphous, with only three developed, and the ovary is many-ovuled. The fruit is an oblong pod, 6 to 15 centimeters long and 2 to 3 centimeters wide, with a thin, crustaceous exocarp and a pulpy, acid, and edible mesocarp. The sampalok is widely planted in the Philippines, often as an ornamental along avenues, and was likely introduced from tropical Africa. It is now pantropic in cultivation.

COMMON USES

The sampalok tree is versatile in its uses across culinary, medicinal, and industrial applications. The fruit is commonly used as a souring condiment, high in vitamins B and C, and is enjoyed fresh, candied, or as a base for jams, sweets, and beverages. In culinary practices, unripe fruit serves as a souring agent in dishes like sinigang and curries, while seeds and young leaves are also consumed in various preparations. Medicinally, the tree's bark, leaves, flowers, and pulp are used in treatments for fevers, digestive issues, and skin conditions, among others. In addition, the tree provides practical benefits: its wood is used for furniture and tools, its leaves and flowers are used in dyeing, and its seeds produce oil for lighting and varnishing. The sampalok also serves as fodder for livestock and nectar for honeybees, showcasing its broad utility and significance.
