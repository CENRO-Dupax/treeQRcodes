BANATO

(Mallotus philippensis)

Banato is a tree that grows between 4 to 10 meters tall, with branchlets, young leaves, and inflorescences covered in brown hairs. Its alternate, oblong-ovate leaves are 7 to 16 centimeters long, with pointed tips and rounded bases, featuring smooth glands on the upper surface and crimson glands on the glaucous, hairy lower surface. Male flowers are small and numerous, growing on axillary spikes 5 to 8 centimeters long, while female flowers grow on solitary racemes 3 to 7 centimeters long. The spherical fruit, 6 to 8 millimeters in diameter, is covered in crimson powder and contains three cells, each with a dark grey, flattened seed.

COMMON USES

Banato has various folkloric uses. Its leaves or seeds are pounded and applied to fungal skin infections, and the red glands of the fruit are known for antiherpetic and anthelmintic properties. The powdered fruit is used for treating skin conditions and intestinal worms. In India, it is used for bronchitis, abdominal diseases, and spleen enlargement. The bark is applied for typhoid, meningitis, and urinary issues. Banato's oil treats skin problems and non-healing wounds, while its bark is a purgative and vulnerary. In traditional dye-making, the ripened fruit glands produce a yellow to orange-red Kamala dye used in textiles and other crafts. Additionally, the wood is used for fuel, paper pulp, and sometimes timber, and the leaves serve as fodder.
