PAGSAHINGIN

(Canarium luzonicum)

The Pagsahingin, part of the Burseraceae family, includes about 75 genera and 550 species, with nine species found in the Philippines. Among them, Canarium luzonicum is significant for its resin, known as Manila elemi, which is a key source of elemi. This resin, obtained from the treeâ€™s bark, is a soft, fragrant, and viscous substance with a honey-like consistency and balsamic odor. Elemi, once used to describe various oleoresins, now specifically refers to the Manila elemi, renowned for its quality. It is collected year-round from Philippine rainforests, with peak yields during the wet season. The resin is extracted through a process of tapping the tree and steam distillation, producing 15% to 25% essential oil. Elemi shares properties with frankincense and is sometimes called "Poor Man's Frankincense," with France, Germany, and Japan being major markets.

COMMON USES

The Pagsahingin has diverse uses. Its seeds can be eaten raw or cooked, and the roasted nuts are enjoyed for their flavor and used in confections and ice creams. However, the kernel coat should be removed to avoid diarrhea. The seeds also yield a sweet oil suitable for cooking. Folklorically, the oleoresin is applied to treat arthritis, boils, burns, and sores, while tree bark is used in postpartum baths and for fevers and chills. The sap is used in various traditional remedies. The resin has additional applications: it serves as an anti-lice treatment, is used in perfumery, and plays a role in aromatherapy for its stimulating and antimicrobial properties. Industrially, it is utilized in varnishes, paints, and various products like soaps, plastics, and shoe polish.
