BALINGHASAI

(Buchanania arborescens)

Balinghasai is an evergreen tree characterized by its minutely yellowish pubescent to nearly glabrous branchlets. The petiole is 2-2.5 centimeters long, supporting a leaf blade that is obovate-oblong to obovate-elliptic, ranging from 8 to 18 centimeters in length and 4 to 6 centimeters in width. The leaves are leathery and glabrous with a cuneate base, entire margin, and rounded apex, featuring 10-15 pairs of prominent lateral veins on both sides. The panicle of the tree is terminal or axillary, measuring 8-10 centimeters and covered in minute ferruginous pubescence. The white flowers have a glabrous calyx with suborbicular lobes and ciliate margins. The petals are elliptic to suborbicular, 3-4 millimeters long, and fleshy. Stamens are equal in length to the petals, with linear to subulate filaments longer than the anthers, which are sagittate. The disk is large and thick. The carpels are five in number, minutely pubescent, and the drupe is lens-shaped, approximately 8 millimeters in size, and glabrous..

COMMON USES

The Balinghasai tree bears edible fruits that are sweet, pulpy, and consumed raw. In traditional practices, Australian aborigines use the plant to treat toothaches by pounding the inner bark and sapwood, soaking it in water, and using the infusion as a mouthwash (but not swallowing). The infusion of the roots is used in a similar manner. Additionally, the plant is employed in traditional medicine to address a range of conditions, including abdominal pains, tumors, boils, coughs, eye infections, gingivitis, and inflammation.
