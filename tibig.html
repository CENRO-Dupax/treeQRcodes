TIBIG

(Ficus nota)

Tibig is a flowering plant in the Moraceae family, known for being dispersed by birds that consume its fruits and excrete the seeds. This perennial tree, reaching heights of 8 to 10 meters, features erect and spreading branches with hairy branchlets. The leaves are oblong to elliptic-obovate, measuring 15 to 35 centimeters long and 8 to 12 centimeters wide, soft and pubescent underneath, with irregularly toothed margins, an abruptly acute apex, and an auriculate base. The midrib is stout, with 7 to 9 pairs of ascending, curved nerves, and the petiole is brown, tomentose, and 3 to 5 centimeters long. The tree has cauline or occasionally larger branch tubercles that are clustered, rebranched, and rigid, up to 20 centimeters long. The figs are subglobose, 2 to 3.5 centimeters in diameter, glabrous, fleshy, and green, turning yellowish-white at the base with exerted umbilical scales and a 2-centimeter-long acute peduncle with three bracts.

COMMON USES

Tibig nota has various uses both in culinary and traditional medicine. Its ripe fruits, though edible, are rather tasteless and are typically consumed with sugar, while young leaves are eaten as vegetables. Folklorically, the plant's water extracted from standing trees is used to treat fever, and the same water is applied to relieve muscle pain. Decoctions of the roots and bark are utilized for urinary tract infections, hypertension, and diabetes, and water from cut branches is used for urinary infections. The Mansaka tribe employs a bark decoction for asthma and respiratory issues, while the Ayta people use the stem as an insect repellent and for toothache and stomachache relief. In Aklan, the Ati tribe uses a root decoction for postpartum care and to enhance milk production. Additionally, the plant's latex is used as a galactagogue. The wood of Ficus nota serves as firewood or charcoal, stems are burned as insect repellent, and the plant is involved in mummification practices for its antibacterial properties. It is also planted along creeks to aid in erosion control.
