Lago

(Prunus grisea)

Lago is a species in the Rosaceae family, first described in 1965 by Cornelis Kalkman. It can grow as a tree or shrub with twigs that are initially pubescent but soon become glabrous. The leaves are typically elliptic to oblong, measuring 2-20 by 1-9 cm, and may be either glabrous or sparsely pubescent on both sides, with 4 to 11 pairs of nerves. The petioles range from 0.25 to 2 cm long, and stipules vary in size, often being glandular. The plant bears racemes of flowers in the axils of leaves, with pedicels up to 7 mm long and a hypanthium that is pubescent outside but sometimes glabrous inside. The flowers have 6-13 perianth segments and numerous stamens. The fruit is globular to ellipsoid, measuring 6-13 by 7-16 mm, and can be glabrous or sparsely hairy, with a glabrous or sparsely hairy endocarp.

COMMON USES

In traditional medicine, Lago has several folkloric uses: the bark is applied to alleviate skin irritation, while the leaves are utilized to treat rheumatism. Additionally, the wood of this tree serves practical purposes in construction, being used for indoor building projects, furniture, plywood, and packing cases.
