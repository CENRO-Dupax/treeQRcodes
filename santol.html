SANTOL

(Sandoricum koetjape)

The santol fruit, previously thought to be two separate species, actually comes in two varieties: yellow and red. The primary distinction lies in the color of the older leaves before they fall, with the red variety being more common and visually striking due to its mix of red and green foliage. The Sandoricum koetjape tree, which can grow up to 20 meters tall, features softly hairy young branches and leaves arranged in groups of three. Its leaves are elliptic to oblong-ovate, 10 to 25 centimeters long, with pointed tips and round bases. The tree produces numerous small flowers in greenish or yellowish clusters. The rounded, somewhat flattened fruits, measuring 4 to 6 centimeters, turn yellowish-orange when ripe and have a thick pericarp. The large seeds are encased in a translucent, pale, and sweet-tasting edible pulp.

COMMON USES

The santol fruit (Sandoricum koetjape) is consumed both raw and spiced, with its high carbohydrate content and fair amounts of iron and vitamin B making it a nutritious choice. It is enjoyed fresh or preserved in syrup, and unripe fruits are used as a souring agent in dishes like sinigang and Thai som tam, as well as in Thai curries with pork and prawns. Folklorically, various parts of the tree have medicinal uses: fresh leaves are sudorific and applied to the skin for various ailments, while the bark and roots are used for conditions like diarrhea, fever, and ringworm.
