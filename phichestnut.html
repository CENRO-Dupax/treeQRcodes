PHILIPPINE CHESTNUT

(Castanopsis)

The Philippine chestnut tree, commonly known as the Philippine Chestnut, is a large evergreen tree native to the Philippines and other parts of Southeast Asia. It typically grows to a height of 20-30 meters, with a trunk diameter reaching up to 60 centimeters. The tree has a straight, cylindrical trunk with dark brown to grayish bark that is often rough and furrowed. The leaves of the Philippine chestnut are alternately arranged, simple, and leathery, with an ovate to lanceolate shape. They are typically 10-20 centimeters long and 4-10 centimeters wide, with serrated or toothed margins and a glossy dark green upper surface. The underside of the leaves is usually a lighter green and may have a slightly hairy texture. The tree produces small, inconspicuous flowers arranged in dense clusters or spikes. These flowers are followed by spiky, greenish-brown fruits that mature into hard, spiny burs containing one to three glossy, brown seeds, known as chestnuts. The fruits are often encased in a prickly husk that splits open when the nuts are ripe. In the Philippines, the Philippine chestnut is valued for its timber, which is used in construction and furniture-making. The tree also provides shade and contributes to soil conservation. Additionally, its nuts are edible and can be used in various culinary applications, though they are not as commonly consumed as other chestnut varieties.

COMMON USES

The Philippine chestnut tree (Castanopsis sp.) is valued for several practical uses. Its timber, known for its durability and strength, is commonly used in construction and furniture-making, offering a reliable material for both structural and decorative applications. The tree also provides excellent shade, making it a popular choice for landscaping and urban greening projects. Additionally, the chestnuts produced by the tree are edible and can be used in various culinary dishes, although they are less commonly consumed compared to other chestnut varieties. The Philippine chestnut tree plays a role in soil conservation by preventing erosion, and its dense foliage contributes to biodiversity by providing habitat for local wildlife.
