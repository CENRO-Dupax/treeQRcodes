MAMALIS

(Pittosporum pentandrum)

Mamalis is a tree occasionally reaching heights of 20 meters, although it is usually much smaller. Whole tree is smooth except for its inflorescence. Leaves are narrowly elliptic, 6 to 15 centimeters long, and less than 2 centimeters wide, and gradually narrowed at both ends. Flowers are white, fragrant, about 6 millimeters long, crowded in panicles 5 to 8 centimeters in length. Fruit is small, globular, pale yellow to orange, somewhat rounded when fresh, 6 to 8 millimeters in diameter. Seeds are about 8, flattened, covered with a glossy red, oily and sticky mucus, with an odor reminiscent of petroleum.

COMMON USES

In folkloric practices, Pittosporum pentandrum has various traditional uses. Women use an aromatic decoction of its leaves for postpartum baths. The Ati tribe in Antique, Philippines, incorporates the plant into postpartum care by boiling its leaves and flowers with other local plants like Canarium sp., J. gendarussa, C. citratus, Glochidion sp., and Bambusa sp. or C. maxima, drinking a glass of the decoction and using the rest for bathing. In Negrito society, the bark is tied around the neck or woven into a necklace to treat coughs and headaches. Additionally, a decoction of the bark is used for fever and cough, with powdered bark serving as an antipyretic and febrifuge, and in larger doses, as a general antidote. The plant is also employed for bronchitis and aromatic baths after childbirth or illness. Its fruit juice and decoction are used for cleansing wounds. Beyond medicinal uses, the wood is utilized for jewelry beads and firewood, while Mamalis oil, obtained from steam distillation of the fruits, yields a pleasantly scented oil.
