AFRICAN TULIP

(Spathodea campanulata)

Spathodea campanulata, commonly known as the African tulip tree, is the only species in the monotypic genus Spathodea, part of the Bignonaceae family. It was first identified by Europeans in 1787 on the Gold Coast of Africa and is considered one of the world's top 100 invasive species due to its widespread cultivation in tropical regions for its vibrant, showy flowers. The tree grows up to 20 meters tall with a dense, dark green crown and pinnately compound leaves. Its large, bell-shaped scarlet or crimson flowers, with yellow frilled edges, are borne on terminal racemes, while its pods are thick and dark brown, containing seeds with broad, silvery wings.

COMMON USES

Spathodea campanulata has various uses, particularly in traditional medicine. Its seeds are reported to be edible, while its bark and leaves are commonly used in decoctions to treat gastrointestinal issues, dysentery, constipation, and skin diseases. In Africa, the plant is used for wound healing, with bruised leaves and flowers applied to ulcers and wounds. It is also used for conditions such as convulsions, malaria, and diabetes, and in Jamaica, the fluid from its flower buds is used for treating glaucoma. Additionally, its bark is used for treating chickenpox and genital herpes. In some regions, the wood is used for making paper and drums, and its seeds are reportedly used as poison by hunters. The plant's flowers yield a natural dye, and children play with its water-filled buds.
