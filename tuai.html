TUAI

(Bischofia javanica)

Tuai is a fast-growing, medium to large tree, reaching heights of 12-50 meters with a dense, rounded crown. Its straight, stout trunk can grow up to 159 cm in diameter, with a short branchless portion extending to 20 meters. The bark is fissured, scaly, and reddish to purplish brown, with a fibrous pink inner bark that exudes red sap. The spirally arranged, compound leaves have three elliptical to ovate leaflets with finely toothed margins. The tree produces unisexual, small, greenish flowers in axillary panicles up to 27 cm long. The fruit is a bluish-black globose drupe, 1-1.5 cm in diameter, with leathery skin and fleshy pulp, containing 3-6 brown, obovoid seeds about 5 mm long.

COMMON USES

Tuai has various uses, both edible and medicinal. The young, soft leaves can be eaten raw or cooked, used in salads, as a vegetable, or as a condiment, and in South Sumatra, they are used as a spice. The fruits are also used to make wine. Medicinally, the fresh bark is used to treat stomachaches, and crushed leaves are applied to relieve stomach pain. In India, the bark is used to treat tuberculosis, body aches, stomach ulcers, mouth ulcers, and inflammatory conditions, while in China, the roots are used to treat rheumatic pains and malaria. The leaves and bark are also used by ethnic communities in Assam and Arunachal Pradesh to treat stomach ailments, gastric afflictions, jaundice, and by Karo healers in Indonesia for diabetes. In Tamil Nadu, the Paliyar tribes mix the stem bark with coconut oil to stimulate hair growth, while in Kenya, a decoction of roots is used to treat stomach infections. Beyond medicinal use, the bark of B. javanica yields a red dye for staining rattan baskets and bamboo mattings, and the inner bark produces a brown dye for coloring tapa bark cloth. The bark also contains about 16% tannin, used for toughening nets and ropes. The wood is medium-weight, moderately hard, and used in construction for beams, posts, decks, bridges, railway sleepers, and more. It is also a potential source of long fibers for pulp and paper production, and its oil serves as a lubricant. Although unsuitable as fuelwood, it is used for charcoal production. Additionally, research suggests that incorporating 15% pintoi peanut meal in mallard duck diets can enhance growth.
