ANILAU

(Commersonia bartramia)

Anilau is a small tree or shrub, reaching up to 20 meters in height, with a trunk diameter of 15-25 cm and flexuous twigs covered in white lenticels. The leaves are spirally arranged, simple, and vary from suborbicular to ovate-oblong, measuring 6-30 cm by 2.5-25 cm, with a cordate or subtruncate base and serrate-dentate or lobed margins. The inflorescence is a corymbose cyme, with bisexual, 5-merous flowers featuring white, ovate sepals and petals, a short staminal tube, and 5 subsessile or stalked stamens. The fruit is a globose, woody capsule, 2-2.5 cm in diameter, with stellate-hairy bristles, and dehisces loculicidally into 5 valves, each containing 1-2 seeds. The leaves have a tomentose lower surface, and the staminodes are pubescent with a tomentose central lobe, while the capsule features stellate-pubescent bristles.

COMMON USES

Anilau has various uses across different regions. While there is no information on the edibility of its fruit, its medicinal applications are noted: in Fuji, Japan, it is used to treat coughs, colds, rheumatism, kidney troubles, and dysentery; in North Sumatra, Indonesia, the bark is used for sprue, and the Batak Toba tribe uses the leaves for malaria and abdominal pain. The bast fiber of the tree is utilized in the Philippines and Indonesia to make ropes, mats, and, historically, fuses; in New Britain, Papua New Guinea, it is used for women's girdles, and Australian Aborigines use it for fish and kangaroo nets. The wood, being light, soft, and close-grained, is used in light construction and fishing floats, and it serves as a good source of firewood, with additional use for making headbands in the Bismarck Archipelago.
