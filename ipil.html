IPIL

(Intsia bijuga)

Ipil is a medium-sized, slow-growing tree that can reach heights of 20 to 45 meters with a trunk diameter of 0.5 to 5 meters. Mature trees feature steep, rounded buttresses. The bark is 5 to 8 millimeters thick, gray with an orange tinge, while the inner bark is light brown with mottled brown specks. The alternate, simply compound leaves usually have two pairs of leaflets measuring 8 to 12 centimeters in length and 5 to 8.5 centimeters in width. Its fragrant flowers, white or reddish, grow in panicles that are 6 to 10 centimeters long. The tree produces oblong or pear-shaped woody pods, 10 to 25 centimeters long and 4 to 6 centimeters wide, containing 3 to 9 orbicular seeds.

COMMON USES

Ipil seeds are edible after being soaked in salt water for 3-4 days and then boiled. The bark, rich in tannin, is used to treat diarrhea and urinary conditions, while the fruit serves as a laxative. In various cultures, ipil bark and leaves are used for ailments such as rheumatism, asthma, muscle rigidity, and diarrhea. The bark is also used post-delivery for women, and in some regions, is believed to treat ailments caused by sorcery. Its hard, durable wood is valued for timber, furniture, and carving, and it repels termites. Additionally, ipil wood yields a khaki dye, while its seed oil can act as an insect repellent. The plant holds cultural significance in rituals and medicinal practices across different regions.
